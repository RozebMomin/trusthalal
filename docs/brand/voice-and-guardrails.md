# Voice & guardrails

> The product exists to *not overstate what we know*. Marketing has to hold the same line, or it
> undercuts the pitch. These rules are canonical. When copy and these rules conflict, the copy is wrong.

## Never

- **No fabricated social proof.** No invented reviews, ratings, testimonials, or star claims ("4.8 stars," "diners love it"). A fake-review store panel was deleted for exactly this reason — don't reintroduce it in captions or graphics.
- **No unearned superlatives.** No "#1," "the best," "guaranteed halal," or "certified halal" applied to a restaurant we only self-attested.
- **No tier laundering.** Never imply we verified a place when its tier is Certificate-on-File or Self-Attested. The tier named in copy must match the tier shown in the app.
- **No green for self-attested.** Emerald is Verified only — including in decorative graphics.
- **No invented numbers.** Don't cite stats ("we've verified 500 restaurants") unless the figure is real and current.

## Always

- **Make the mechanic the star.** The three tiers, the evidence, and the honesty of admitting when we don't know *are* the story.
- **Disclose plainly.** "Every paid meal disclosed" is a brag here, not fine print.
- **Sell the feeling, honestly.** The emotional hook is the move from *guessing* to *knowing*. It's true, so lean on it.
- **Match the app.** Colours, tier names, and claims in any asset must reflect what the product actually shows.

## Why this is strict

The whole reason a diner would trust us over a "HALAL" sign in a window is that we *don't* launder the owner's word into our endorsement. The moment our marketing overstates, we become the thing we're differentiating against. Honesty isn't a tone here — it's the product.
