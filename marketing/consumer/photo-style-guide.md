# Photography Style Guide

The single biggest visual lever for this brand. Real, warm, specific. Photos make Trust Halal feel like a community guide instead of a tech app.

## The bar

Every photo on the site or in social should be:

* **Real food from a real restaurant** — not staged food photography
* **Warm in tone** — natural light, no over-saturated filters
* **Culturally specific** — Yemeni mandi looks like Yemeni mandi, not generic "Middle Eastern food"
* **Honest about scale and setting** — a halal kebab joint in Brooklyn looks like a halal kebab joint in Brooklyn

## The look — what to shoot

### Hero shots (used on landing, restaurant detail pages)

* **Subject:** the food itself, or food + hands holding it.
* **Composition:** overhead (food on table) or eye-level (food on a counter, food in hands).
* **Light:** window light. Natural, side-lit. No overhead fluorescents. Time of day: late morning or late afternoon (the "golden hour" for indoor restaurant light).
* **Plates and props:** whatever the restaurant actually uses. Don't bring linen napkins or styled sides. The plate should look like one a diner would be served.
* **People:** hands, partial faces, the cook reaching across the counter. Avoid full posed portraits — those read as a campaign, not a moment.

### Restaurant exterior / interior shots

* **Subject:** the storefront, the sign, the seating, the counter.
* **Composition:** eye-level mostly. The verified window sticker (when applied) should be visible in at least one shot per restaurant.
* **Light:** natural daylight for exteriors. Available light for interiors — no harsh flash.
* **No "empty restaurant" shots.** Either at a quiet moment with one or two real diners, or staged with the owner / staff visible. Empty rooms read as closed/abandoned.

### Detail shots (textures, certs, the human moments)

* The halal certificate on the wall where it actually hangs.
* The cook's hands at work — flipping, plating, garnishing.
* The handwritten daily-special board.
* Steam rising off a fresh dish.
* A diner taking a first bite (always with permission).

## The look — what to avoid

* **Over-saturated, over-filtered "Instagram preset" looks.** Trust Halal is warm but not artificial.
* **Generic stock food photography:** the slate background with crossed forks, the dramatic dark moody overhead with herbs scattered around the plate. We're a directory of real places, not a food magazine.
* **AI-generated food images of real restaurants.** They have a tell, and the moment a viewer notices it, the entire trust story collapses.
* **Over-styled flat-lays.** The 8-things-arranged-in-a-grid composition feels editorial, not human.
* **Cultural appropriation shots:** non-Muslim hands in the frame "trying" the food, exoticizing captions, etc.
* **Stereotypical "ethnic food" props:** woven baskets, brass coins, Islamic calligraphy used as backdrop decoration. Pure aestheticization — disrespectful and shallow.

## Source hierarchy

### 1. Best: the restaurant's own photos

* Most owners have a phone camera and a friend with one.
* Ask for photos when you onboard them. Make it part of the verification process — "Could you send us 3–5 of your favorite recent food photos from your restaurant?"
* Offer to credit them and to post the photo with attribution.

### 2. Good: photographer on assignment

* If you launch in a specific city and have budget, hire a local food photographer for a 2-day shoot. 10–15 verified restaurants, $1,500–$3,000 total. Worth it for the hero / monthly-feature content.
* Brief the photographer with this style guide. Their default is probably food-magazine style; you want documentary-natural.

### 3. Acceptable: stock photography (with care)

* Unsplash and Pexels have decent halal-friendly food photography under permissive licenses.
* Search terms that work: "biryani", "kebab", "shawarma", "mandi", "tagine", "kunafa", "samosa", "kofta", "doner", "halal restaurant"
* Always credit the photographer in the page's image metadata (alt text or hover caption).
* Acceptable for: SEO page hero images, blog content, social posts about a cuisine in general.
* NOT acceptable for: a specific verified restaurant's listing page (always use real photos of the actual place).

### 4. Last resort: AI-generated imagery

* Acceptable for: mood-board work, internal mockups, placeholder images during development.
* Acceptable for: stylized social posts where the image is clearly illustrative (geometric, badge-style, etc.) — not depicting real food.
* NEVER acceptable for: anything that depicts a specific restaurant, a specific dish at a real place, or anything that could be mistaken for a photo of an actual meal. Loss of trust is permanent.

## Photo specs

| Use | Aspect | Min size |
|---|---|---|
| Landing hero | 16:9 or 4:3 | 2400 × 1350 |
| Restaurant detail hero | 3:2 | 1800 × 1200 |
| Restaurant card thumbnail | 4:3 | 800 × 600 |
| Social feed (IG) | 1:1 or 4:5 | 1080 × 1080 / 1080 × 1350 |
| Social story / reel | 9:16 | 1080 × 1920 |
| Email featured image | 16:9 | 1200 × 675 |

Always export as WebP (with JPEG fallback) for site delivery. Sharing files for social can stay as JPG.

## File naming convention

Use the format: `restaurant-slug_dish-or-context_date.webp`

Examples:
* `karims-houston_lamb-biryani_2026-03.webp`
* `karims-houston_storefront_2026-03.webp`
* `karims-houston_certificate-on-wall_2026-03.webp`

Makes it easy to find images later and to credit the right restaurant on social shares.

## Permission and credit

* **Always credit photographers.** In the image's caption or alt text. If the restaurant owner took the photo, credit "Photo: [Owner Name] / [Restaurant Name]."
* **Get explicit permission for people in photos.** If you can see someone's face, ask. A nod is fine for staff at work; signed releases are safer for featured close-ups.
* **Children:** never photograph children at restaurants for marketing without explicit signed parental consent. Easier to just not.
* **Religious garments:** the same rules apply as any other portrait. Don't single out hijabs/beards/kufis as visual flavoring; treat the wearer the same as any other subject.

## Tool recommendations

* **AI placeholders during build:** Midjourney v6 or Flux Pro both produce passable food imagery, though it always has the AI tell once you know what to look for. Use for layout testing, not for live content.
* **Real photo editing:** Lightroom Mobile (free tier is fine) or Snapseed (free) for color and exposure. Avoid heavy filters or presets — the brand's warm tone should come from the actual photo, not a filter overlay.
* **Stock sources:** Unsplash (free, no attribution required but appreciated), Pexels (free, attribution appreciated). Avoid Shutterstock — too generic, and you'd be paying for photos that competitors are using too.
* **Photographer directories:** Sohag (Muslim-led photography network), Voyaged (food + travel photographers), or just Instagram DM a local food photographer whose work you like.

## The one-question check

Before publishing any photo, ask:

> Does this photo make Trust Halal feel like a community guide written by people who actually eat at these restaurants — or does it make it feel like a SaaS landing page?

If the second, find a different photo.
