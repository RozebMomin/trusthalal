# Consumer Landing Copy — halalfoodnearme.com

Copy for the consumer-facing landing surface. Targets the halal-conscious diner: observant Muslims, families raising halal kids, anyone keeping a strict diet. Warm voice; acknowledges the pain of finding actually-halal restaurants; specific about what verification means.

Designed for a single-scroll mobile-first experience.

**Body** (one paragraph, ~50 words)
> The "halal" sign in the window. The Yelp review that said yes. The waiter who said the chicken was zabihah but the kitchen used the same oil. We get it — every halal-conscious diner has a story. Trust Halal exists because the stories shouldn't keep happening.

## Section 3 — What "verified" actually means

**Section eyebrow** (small olive caps)
> WHAT WE CHECK

**Headline**
> Verified means we did the asking. So you don't have to.

**Body intro** (one sentence)
> Every verified restaurant has a public halal profile with the specifics — no vague "halal options available."

**4 cards, 2x2 grid:**

**Card 1 — Per-product meat sourcing 🥩**
> Beef from one supplier, chicken from another, lamb you don't carry — we capture the actual supplier name and slaughter method for every meat on the menu. Zabihah, machine, or none.

**Card 2 — Menu posture 🍽️**
> Fully halal? Halal options on a mixed menu? Separate kitchens? Pork on the menu? You see what's true about the kitchen — and you filter for what you personally accept.

**Card 3 — Certification on file 📄**
> Restaurants that carry a halal certificate (IFANCA, HFSAA, HMS, local mosque, etc.) — we capture it. You can view and download the actual cert from the restaurant's listing.

**Card 4 — Alcohol policy 🍷**
> None? Beer and wine only? Cooking with wine reductions? You see the honest answer in advance.

## Copy notes

* No exclamation points. The voice is steady, not enthusiastic.
* "Halal" appears throughout in normal sentence case — never SHOUTED, never quoted ("halal").
* The community-first framing in section 7 is load-bearing. Don't cut it for layout reasons.
* The empathy section ("You've probably been let down before") is doing real work — it's the moment where the reader recognizes the brand understands them. Don't soften it.
* Specific city names in the trust-signal row are stronger than "across the US." If you don't have 5 cities yet, just list the 1–2 you have plus "and more coming."
* The CTA hierarchy: search bar (primary) → trust-tier filter (mid) → email list (soft). Don't multiply CTAs.
* Avoid testimonials / star ratings on the landing page until you have at least 20 with real names attached. A handful of fake-feeling quotes hurts trust more than no quotes at all.

---

## Mobile considerations

* The hero collapses to: headline, subhead, search bar, "Near me" button. The phone visual disappears or moves below.
* The 2x2 verification cards become a single column.
* The trust-tier cards become a horizontally-scrolling carousel.
* The community partner logo row collapses to a 3-wide grid.

The page should fit comfortably on a 375px viewport (iPhone SE) without horizontal scroll.

---

## A/B test ideas (for later)

* **Hero headline:** "Find a halal restaurant your gut can trust." vs. "Find halal restaurants without the guesswork."
* **Empathy section:** with/without the "You've probably been let down before" framing — does it convert better or worse than a more neutral opener?
* **Trust signal row:** numbers vs. a verified-restaurant grid (logos/names). Logos are more credible once you have ~10 well-known names.

Don't run these tests until you have at least 1k visitors/week — earlier and the data is too noisy to mean anything.
