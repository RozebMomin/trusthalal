# Community Partnership Outreach

Free distribution if you do it right. The Muslim community runs on word of mouth, mosque announcements, MSA Slack channels, and trusted recommendations from imams, community leaders, and food influencers. None of that is a marketing channel you can buy — but it's all available if you reach out respectfully.

This file has templates for:
1. Mosque outreach
2. MSA / Muslim student organization outreach
3. Halal food influencer outreach
4. Imam / community leader 1:1 introductions

---

## 1. Mosque outreach

The mosque is the community hub. A 30-second mention from the minbar after Friday prayers, or a flyer in the lobby, can reach hundreds of people in a single weekend — and reach them with trust that no ad can buy.

### Email template — mosque admin / outreach committee

**Subject:** A community resource for finding halal restaurants — would this be useful at [Mosque Name]?

**Body:**

> Assalamu alaikum,
>
> I'm reaching out from Trust Halal — we're a Muslim-led platform that verifies halal restaurants (supplier, slaughter method, certificate on file) so the community can find places to eat without having to call ahead.
>
> The website is halalfoodnearme.com. There are already [N] verified restaurants in [City], with more being added every week.
>
> I wanted to ask: would your congregation find it useful to know this exists? A few ways we could share — pick whichever (if any) feels right:
>
> * A card/flyer in the lobby (we'll mail you a stack)
> * A line in the weekly bulletin or e-newsletter (we'll send a few options to choose from)
> * A 30-second mention after Jumu'ah (we'll provide a script)
> * Linking from your mosque's website resources page
>
> No expectation either way. If it's not a fit, please ignore. If it is, I'd be happy to call or visit in person to explain more.
>
> Jazakum Allah khairan for your time.
>
> Wassalam,
> [Your name]
> Trust Halal
> hello@trusthalal.org

### Flyer copy — lobby version

A simple half-sheet you can mail to mosques. Headline + 3 lines + URL + QR.

**Headline:**
> Find halal restaurants you can actually trust.

**Body:**
> Trust Halal verifies halal restaurants — supplier, slaughter method, certificate on file. Built by and for the community.

**CTA:**
> halalfoodnearme.com

**QR code** sized 1.5 inches, lower-right. Olive band along the top with the wordmark.

### Friday announcement script (30 seconds)

For the imam or announcement coordinator. Provide this verbatim — they'll adapt.

> "InshAllah, before we go, a quick note for our community: there's a new community resource called Trust Halal — a directory of verified halal restaurants. The site lists places where the supplier, slaughter method, and certificate have all been checked. It's at halal-food-near-me dot com. May Allah bless the team who put it together and continue to make finding halal food easier for our community. Ameen."

### Newsletter / bulletin one-paragraph

Two versions — pick whichever fits your mosque's voice:

**Brief version (under 60 words):**

> **Trust Halal — verified halal restaurants.** A new community resource is live: halalfoodnearme.com. Trust Halal lists restaurants where the supplier, slaughter method, and certification have all been verified. Free to use. Built by community members for the community. Worth a bookmark for the next time you're trying to find a halal place you can trust.

**Longer version (~100 words):**

> **A new community resource for finding halal food.** Trust Halal (halalfoodnearme.com) is a new directory of verified halal restaurants. Unlike Yelp or Google, where halal claims are unverified, Trust Halal lists what's actually checked — supplier name, slaughter method, certificate on file, alcohol policy — for every restaurant. The platform is Muslim-led, free to use, and free for restaurants to be verified. It currently covers [City] with more cities rolling out as community waitlists grow. If you've ever had to call ahead to ask whether a place's chicken is actually zabihah, this is for you.

## 2. MSA / Muslim student organization outreach

Campuses are dense, social, and trust-driven. The MSA listserv or Slack channel reaches every Muslim student on campus instantly. Two posts a semester is plenty.

### Email template — MSA president or social media chair

**Subject:** Halal restaurant resource for the [University] MSA — useful?

**Body:**

> Assalamu alaikum [name, if known, otherwise "MSA team"],
>
> Hope your semester is going well.
>
> Quick intro: I'm with Trust Halal, a Muslim-led platform that verifies halal restaurants (halalfoodnearme.com). We list restaurants where the supplier, slaughter method, and certificate are all on the public profile — no more "is this actually halal" guessing.
>
> Asking: would the [University] MSA be interested in sharing it with members? Some ideas:
>
> * A post in the MSA group chat / Slack / Discord
> * A line on the MSA's "Halal food on campus" page
> * A handout we can mail for tabling events
>
> No pressure — and we'd love to know how to make this more useful for students if you have suggestions.
>
> Jazakum Allah khairan,
> [Your name]
> Trust Halal

### Slack / Discord / GroupMe one-liner

> Heads up — there's a directory of verified halal restaurants in [City] called Trust Halal: halalfoodnearme.com. Lists supplier, slaughter method, and cert for every place. Useful for finding a spot for an MSA dinner or just for figuring out where to actually eat. Free.

## 3. Halal food influencer outreach

There's a small but real ecosystem of Muslim food influencers on Instagram and TikTok. A single tagged post from one of them can drive 500–2,000 site visits.

### Identify the right influencers

Don't blast every halal food account. Look for:
* **Local** — they cover your launch city or region.
* **Mid-tier** — 10k–250k followers. Big enough to matter, small enough to actually respond and care.
* **Authentic voice** — they post recommendations, not sponcon spam.
* **Active engagement** — their followers actually comment, not just like.

Start a list. Build it to ~50 names. Reach out to 5–10 per week.

### DM template — Instagram

Keep it SHORT. Influencers get inundated.

> Salaam [name]! Love your [specific recent post — be real about it, name what it was]. Reaching out from Trust Halal — we built a directory of verified halal restaurants at halalfoodnearme.com. Wanted to see if you'd be open to checking it out and (no pressure) maybe sharing if you find it useful. Happy to do a paid collab if that's how you usually work. Either way — jazak Allah khair for what you do for the community.

### Email template — for larger influencers / agents

**Subject:** Partnership inquiry — Trust Halal x [Influencer Name]

**Body:**

> Hi [Name],
>
> I run partnerships at Trust Halal, a Muslim-led platform that verifies halal restaurants (halalfoodnearme.com). We're early-stage but growing — currently active in [City] with strong word-of-mouth in the community.
>
> Reaching out because [Influencer Name]'s content is exactly the kind of voice we'd love to align with — particularly [specific reason: their detailed restaurant reviews, their focus on halal sourcing transparency, etc.].
>
> Two possible ways to collaborate:
>
> 1. **Honest review.** Free try of the platform, plus a couple of restaurant comps if you'd like. No script — just your honest take. Best if the content fits your existing posting style.
>
> 2. **Paid partnership.** If a formal sponsorship is more your speed, we can discuss rates. We don't have a big budget yet — but we have flexibility and we move fast.
>
> Either direction, happy to share more details or jump on a call. Thanks for considering it.
>
> [Your name]
> Trust Halal
> hello@trusthalal.org

### What to OFFER an influencer

* Early access to verified-restaurant data.
* A custom referral link they can share.
* Comped meals at 2–3 of the most popular verified restaurants (we comp them; restaurant gets exposure).
* A "Trust Halal recommended by [Influencer Name]" badge on listings they highlight.
* For paid partnerships at this stage: probably $200–$1,500 per post depending on tier. Negotiate up only if their engagement metrics support it.

### What to AVOID

* Don't gift them on day one. Build relationship first; ask favors second.
* Don't write their content for them. Provide context; let them voice it.
* Don't paper-over a bad review. If an influencer tries the product and posts honestly that it didn't meet their needs, take it on the chin and ask what could be better. Don't get defensive in the comments.
* Don't try to enforce specific hashtags / mentions. Suggest, never require.

## 4. Imam / community leader 1:1 introductions

The highest-leverage relationship in the Muslim community is the imam or respected community leader who actively recommends things to the congregation. These are NOT marketing relationships — they're community relationships. Treat them accordingly.

### How to approach

* **In person, not email.** Visit a mosque. Attend a Friday prayer. After prayer, introduce yourself respectfully and ask if they have a few minutes.
* **Frame it as service, not pitch.** "We built this resource for the community — wanted to know if you think it could be useful, and what we could do better."
* **Don't lead with the URL.** Lead with the problem and the principle: trust, transparency, community-built.
* **Bring something physical:** the sales sheet (`owner/sales-sheet.md`), printed. People remember what they hold.

### The actual conversation

> "Assalamu alaikum. My name is [your name] — I'm part of a small team that built a platform called Trust Halal, a directory of verified halal restaurants. The reason I came to introduce myself is that the community is the reason this exists, and we wanted to make sure leaders like yourself knew about it. Most halal-conscious diners have a story about being told a place is halal and finding out it wasn't — we wanted to try to fix that. I'm not here to ask for an endorsement — just to introduce ourselves and see if you'd find it useful for the congregation."

That's the conversation. If they're warm, follow up with how they'd like to engage. If they're neutral, thank them and move on. If they're skeptical, listen carefully — they're often picking up on something genuine you should hear.

### Mistakes to avoid

* Don't pitch like a salesperson. Imams have been pitched on a thousand things. They can smell it.
* Don't promise things you can't deliver ("we'll feature your mosque on the homepage").
* Don't use religious language as a marketing tool ("InshaAllah Trust Halal will revolutionize..." — overdoing it reads as fake).
* Don't try to convert a single conversation into a deal. The relationship is the deal. Show up over time.

## Tracking and following up

Keep a single spreadsheet:

| Date | Org/Person | Channel | Contact | Response | Status | Next step |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

After 50 outreaches you'll see which channels actually work for your launch city. Probably 1–2 will dominate. Double down there.

## A note on tone

Community outreach lives or dies on tone. The over-rule for every template above: write like a person from the community talking to other people in the community. The minute it starts to sound like marketing, you've lost.

Read every email aloud before sending. If you'd be embarrassed for your dad to hear you read it, rewrite it.
