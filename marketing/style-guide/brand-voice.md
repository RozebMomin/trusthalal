# Brand Voice Guide

## The one-liner

Trust Halal sounds like a trustworthy friend from your own community who happens to know every halal restaurant in town. Warm. Specific. Honest. Never preachy, never corporate, never pandering.

## Voice principles

**1. Respect comes before cleverness.**
Halal is a religious concept, not a marketing buzzword. We never use "halal" as a punchline or as exotic flavoring for the brand. We use it the way the community uses it — as a meaningful designation that affects what people actually eat.

**2. Acknowledge that trust has been broken before.**
Most Muslim consumers have a story about a restaurant that claimed halal and turned out not to be. Our voice acknowledges that pain instead of pretending it doesn't exist. Lines like "you shouldn't have to call the restaurant first" or "you've probably been let down before" do a lot of work.

**3. Specific beats general.**
Vague language sounds like every other food app. Specific language sounds like us. "Zabihah chicken from a Detroit supplier" beats "100% halal!" every time. Numbers, names, and concrete details signal that we did the work.

**4. Community language, not corporate language.**
"Our communities," "fellow Muslims," "your neighborhood," "the food we grew up eating." Avoid: "users," "customers," "user base," "stakeholders," "value-add."

**5. Confidence without overclaiming.**
We're a small, growing platform. We don't say "the world's #1 halal app" or "every halal restaurant in America." We say what's true: "verified halal restaurants in [your city]" or "the restaurants we've personally checked." Specificity is more credible than scale.

## Vocabulary — preferred

| Use this | Not this |
|---|---|
| Halal-conscious diner | Halal consumer |
| Verified restaurant | Listed restaurant |
| The community | Our user base |
| Trust Halal Verified | Halal-certified (we're not the certifier) |
| Owner / restaurateur | Restaurant client |
| Find a halal place near you | Search our database |
| Submitted for review | Pending |
| We checked | We validated |
| Heads-up | Disclaimer |

## Vocabulary — avoid

* "Disrupt" — we're not disrupting anything; we're filling a gap.
* "Frictionless," "seamless," "best-in-class" — corporate filler.
* "Hidden gem" — overused, especially in food media. Be more specific.
* "Authentic" — loaded word; let restaurants speak for themselves.
* Any joke involving prayer, the Qur'an, or religious practice. Off-limits in all marketing.
* Any use of "exotic" to describe Muslim or halal food.
* "Tribe," "fam," "queens/kings" — too casual for this brand.

## Sentence-level moves we use a lot

**Acknowledge the problem before the pitch.**
> You've probably had to call ahead to ask whether the chicken's actually zabihah. We did the asking for you.

**Show, don't claim.**
> Every Verified restaurant has a halal profile you can scroll through — supplier names, slaughter method per meat, certificate on file. No vague "halal options available."

**Speak to the community directly.**
> Whether you keep strictly zabihah or you're fine with any halal source, the filter lets you set your own line. We don't decide for you.

## How long should things be?

* **Headline:** 6–10 words. Direct, no cleverness for cleverness's sake.
* **Subhead:** one sentence, 15–25 words. Backs up the headline.
* **Body paragraph:** 2–4 sentences. End each with the most important word.
* **Social caption:** 25–60 words for a feed post, ~140 chars for a hook line.
* **CTA button copy:** 2–4 words, verb-led ("Find halal nearby," "Get verified," not "Click here").

## Tone calibration by audience

| Surface | Tone |
|---|---|
| Consumer landing / search | Warm, plainspoken. Address the diner directly: "you," "your." |
| Owner landing / outreach | Peer-to-peer. Talk to owners like fellow community members, not prospects. |
| Press release / formal | Slightly more measured but still warm. Less "you," more "the platform." |
| Mosque / community partnership | Respectful, brief. Less marketing, more "here's what we do, would this be useful for your community?" |
| Social (IG/TikTok) | Punchier, more visual. The voice is still warm — just compressed. |

## A few quick examples — voice in action

**Consumer hero (good):**
> Find a halal restaurant your gut can trust. We verify what's on the plate so you don't have to call the kitchen first.

**Consumer hero (bad):**
> The #1 platform for halal food discovery! Search thousands of authentic options now! 🍽️✨

**Owner pitch (good):**
> If you've been getting "is this actually halal?" calls every week, get verified once. The badge does the answering for you.

**Owner pitch (bad):**
> Boost your visibility on the world's leading halal restaurant platform! Sign up today!

**Mosque outreach (good):**
> We're putting together a directory of restaurants the community can actually trust. If you'd like, we can share a card with your congregation — no pressure, just thought it might be useful.

**Mosque outreach (bad):**
> We have an exciting partnership opportunity for your mosque!

## Things to never do

* Joke about Allah, prayer, the Qur'an, hijab, beards, or any religious observance.
* Use halal as a flavor descriptor ("our halal-flavored marketing").
* Imply that non-Muslims using the app are "trying out" or "exploring" Muslim food. They're just eating.
* Call out specific certifying bodies negatively (no comparing IFANCA to HFSAA, etc.). Stay neutral on bodies; advocate for transparency.
* Use sad/preachy framing ("In a world where halal is hard to find..."). Confident, not pleading.
* Engage in halal-vs-zabihah debates in marketing. Acknowledge both, let users filter.

## Quick gut-check before publishing

Before any piece of copy ships, ask:
1. Would my actual aunt nod at this, or would she raise an eyebrow?
2. Does it sound like a person from the community, or like a brand from a Silicon Valley accelerator?
3. Is there at least one specific detail (a name, a number, a place) doing real work?
4. If a non-Muslim reads it, do they understand without feeling lectured?

If all four checks pass, ship it.
